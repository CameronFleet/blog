# Cameron Fleet — Writing Voice Guide

This file defines Cameron's writing style. Reference it when drafting blog posts.

---

## The goal of every post

Leave the reader with two things: **a model they didn't have before** and **the energy to go do something with it**. Not just information — a shift in how they see the problem, plus momentum.

---

## Voice in one sentence

Opinionated practitioner who explains things from first principles, writes casually, and earns the reader's trust by not talking down to them.

---

## Opening

**Lead with the claim.** No warmup, no scene-setting paragraph, no "in recent years...". The first sentence should be the thing you actually think. If you're not willing to put your take in the first line, the post probably isn't ready.

> ✓ "CLAUDE.md is config. We should treat it like config."
> ✗ "As AI tools become more common in software development, teams are increasingly thinking about..."

---

## Structure and density

- **Varied paragraph length.** Short punchy lines when landing a point. Longer when building an argument. Never uniform — uniform is boring.
- **Short sentences do the hitting.** Save long sentences for when you're holding multiple ideas in tension.
- No fluffy transition paragraphs. If you need to say "Now let's look at...", cut it and just look at it.

---

## Technical depth

Start from **first principles**, even for a technical audience. Assume the reader is smart and has a base level of understanding — but don't assume they've thought about this specific thing before.

- Build up the concept before you deploy the jargon.
- Explain *why* something works, not just *that* it works.
- The goal is genuine understanding, not credentialing yourself.

Influences here: Charity Majors and Will Larson — practitioners who have strong opinions and explain the reasoning, not just the conclusion.

---

## Tone

Mostly direct and serious. **Occasional sharp aside** — a dry line, an understated observation — but never trying too hard to be funny. The wit lives in the understatement, not the punchline.

Cameron writes like he's talking to a smart colleague, not presenting at a conference.

---

## Register

Full contractions. Casual vocabulary. It should feel like it was written by a human, for a human. "It's", "don't", "you're", "we". Not "one should consider".

Swearing: rarely, only if it genuinely lands harder than the alternative.

---

## Closing

**Restate + forward.** End by landing the core thesis cleanly — one or two sentences — then point to what it implies or what comes next. Don't summarise the whole post. Don't trail off. Give the reader somewhere to go.

---

## What to avoid

- Hedging the opening to be safe ("some might argue...", "it depends...")
- Passive voice as a way to avoid taking a position
- Over-explaining what you're about to do instead of doing it
- Lists where prose would flow better
- Ending on a question when you actually have an answer

---

## Comp voices

- **Charity Majors** — strong practitioner takes, earned through doing the thing
- **Will Larson** — structured thinking, engineering leadership, doesn't oversimplify
